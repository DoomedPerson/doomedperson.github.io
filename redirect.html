Spectrographic Analysis of DC Plasma: Mapping Voltage, Pressure, and Temperature Relationships for Optimized Applications
By: Lucius Carr, Christopher Boggs, Mwanza Lungu, John Edwards, Caleb Kasero

This webpage holds similar information to our research poster, however in a written format with more space for graphs and deeper explanations of our methodology.

Theory

DC plasma generators use high-voltage cathodes and anodes to accelerate the light electrons to very high speeds. These high-speed electrons collide with and excite neutral particles. While many factors influence plasmas' formation and subsequent properties, we are particularly interested in the effect of voltage and pressure. The kinetic energy (KE) change of an electron in an electric field is given by \( \color{red}{\Delta KE = e\Delta V} \). This shows that increasing the voltage of our system should increase the speed and, likewise, the ionizing potential of electrons in the plasma. It is hypothesized that increasing the voltage will increase the magnitude of our peaks, however, it could also decrease the magnitude as the ionization energy starts favoring different transitions. Likewise, altering the pressure of the gas should have an impact on the plasma, particularly in how the energy is distributed. This is due to factors such as the mean free path of the electron being altered as the pressure of the gas changes. It can be shown that \(\color{red}{\lambda_{mfp} \propto \frac{P}{T}} \). It is hypothesized that decreasing pressure will initially strengthen the plasmas’ peaks, as it forms, and then subsequently drop in intensity when the pressure gets very low.

The principle behind estimating the electron temperature (\(T_e\)​) in a plasma using spectroscopy is based on the interaction between electrons and atoms in the discharge. In a DC glow discharge, the electrons in the plasma collide with neutral atoms, exciting them to higher energy states. These excited atoms then emit light as they return to lower energy states. The intensity of these spectral lines is related to the population of the excited states, which in turn depends on the electron energy distribution in the plasma. Under the assumption of a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution for the electrons, the population of excited states is given by the Boltzmann factor, \(\color{white}{n_k∝g_{k}e^{-Ek/k_BT_e}}\)​​ where \(n_k​\) is the population of an excited state \(k\), \(g_k\)​ is the degeneracy factor, \(E_k\) is the energy of the excited state, \(k_B\)​ is Boltzmann's constant, and \(T_e\) is the electron temperature.

The intensity of a spectral line, \(I_{ik}\)​, corresponding to a transition from an upper level \(k\) to a lower level \(i\), is proportional to the product of the population of the excited states and the transition probability. Mathematically, the intensity can be written as:

\(I_{ik}=\frac{\hbar cA_{ki}}{2λ_{ki}}\)

where \(\hbar\) is the reduced Planck's constant, \(c\) is the speed of light, \(A_{ki}\)​ is the transition probability, and \(λ_{ki}\) is the wavelength of the emitted light. Substituting the expression for \(n_k\) into this equation and taking the natural logarithm gives the equation for a Boltzmann plot:

\(\ln[\frac{λ_{ki}I_{ki}}{g_kA_{ki}}]=(-\frac{E_k}{k_BT_e})+C\)

where \(C\) is a constant that includes factors like the spectrometer response and plasma length. By plotting the logarithm of the intensity versus the energy of the upper level, the slope of the resulting line gives the electron temperature, \(T_e\)​, from which the temperature can be determined by using the relation: \(T_e=-\frac{1}{k_B*\text{slope}}\). This method allows the estimation of the electron temperature from the spectral intensities of the emission lines.

Methods

Our procedure was generally straight-forward. We used an OceanView spectrometer to measure the spectral intensity between 650-1050nm wavelengths, while tweaking factors such as voltage and pressure. We tweaked these factors independently. We measured at the Anode and Cathode, and took some measurements at different striations. To measure temperature, we utilized the NIST database to find energy, transition probability, and degeneracy for different wavelengths of Argon present in our sample. This data was used to perform our Boltzmann Plot calculations.
